  • Q - Algorithms for resolving the coefficients of polynomial phase of non-linear chirps

  • A1 - ESPRIT for Polynomial-Phase Signals (PP-ESPRIT)

    Idea. Successive finite differencing of a polynomial-phase signal turns it into a cisoid with a Vandermonde structure; then apply ESPRIT to get coefficients in closed form.

    Sketch for a second-order chirp

    1. First difference: .
    2. Form a Hankel/Toeplitz data matrix from Δx[n]x[n], compute its signal subspace (SVD).
    3. Apply ESPRIT shift-invariance to obtain the cisoid “frequency”. Its slope across n yields α, and the intercept gives f0.
    4. Estimate A, ϕ0 by LS on x[n] with known α, f0.

    Pros. Super-resolution, no grid search, excellent for short data records.

    Cons. Needs decent SNR and careful model order (typically 1).

  • A2 - Phase Unwrapping + Weighted Least Squares (WLS)

    Idea. Unwrap the instantaneous phase and fit a quadratic directly.

    1. Compute ϕ̂[n] = arg x[n] and unwrap.
    2. Solve

    minϕ0, f0, α ∑nw[n](ϕ̂[n] − (ϕ0 + 2πf0n + παn2))2,

    with robust weights w[n] (e.g., Tukey/Huber).

    1. Closed-form via normal equations or QR.

    Pros. Analytic, fast, no transforms.

    Cons. Needs good SNR/initialization; use smoothing or short-time averaging before unwrapping.

  • A3 - Fractional Fourier Transform (FrFT) Ridge Maximization

    Idea. A linear FM becomes a tone after a rotation of the time-frequency plane; maximize concentration to read off α.

    1. Sweep FrFT order aa (rotation θ = aπ/2) and find a⋆ that maximizes spectral sharpness (e.g., kurtosis/peakness) of ℱa{x}|.
    2. Convert a⋆a^to chirp rate αvia the FrFT convention you use (the α ↔︎ θ formula depends on your normalization).
    3. De-chirp and estimate f0, ϕ0 as in #1.

    Pros. Very intuitive and robust for broad-band chirps.

    Cons. Be meticulous about FrFT normalization to map θto αcorrectly.

  • A4 - Radon/Ambiguity-Domain Hough Methods (RAT/Radon-TFD)

    Idea. The quadratic chirp ridge is a curve in the TFD or ambiguity plane. Radon/Hough integration along candidate curves yields a sharply peaked accumulator at the true α.

    • Build Wigner–Ville (smoothed) or spectrogram.
    • Integrate energy along quadratic curves parameterized by α; the maximizer gives α.
    • Then de-chirp and estimate f0, ϕ0.

    Pros. High resolution; resilient to moderate noise and partial occlusions.

    Cons. Heavier compute unless you use multiscale/coarse-to-fine search.

    Practical, no-nonsense recipe (works great)

    1. **PHAF for α*: pick a small set of lags τ∈{2,3,4}{2,3,4}, compute PHAF, take the dominant peak → α^.
    2. De-chirp: z[n]=x[n]e−jπαn2z[n]=x[n]e{-jn^2}.
    3. ESPRIT or Prony on z[n]z[n]: get super-resolved f^0f_0.
    4. Amplitude/phase LS: solve z[n]≈Aej(ϕ0+2πf0n)z[n]A e^{j(_0+2f_0 n)} for A,ϕ0A,_0.

    This gives all coefficients analytically, typically with far better than FFT-bin accuracy from short data.

    Implementation tips

    • Windowing & centering: center nn around zero (use n=−N/2…N/2−1n=-N/2N/2-1) to reduce coefficient coupling.
    • Sampling units: keep a consistent normalized frequency (cycles/sample) vs rad/sample; παn2n^2 vs α2n2 n^2 conventions can trip you up.
    • Noise: average PHAF over multiple τ; use robust WLS in the unwrapping method.
    • Multiple components: apply deflation—estimate strongest, subtract, then repeat; or use subspace multi-tone extensions.

    If you want, I can drop in compact pseudocode for PHAF+ESPRIT, or tailor the steps to your sampling rate/record length and expected SNR.
